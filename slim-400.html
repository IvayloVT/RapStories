Biography

Early Life and Bompton Identity

Vincent Cohran, known professionally as Slim 400, was born on March 21, 1988, and became closely associated with Compton, California—more specifically with Bompton, a local identity and street reality that shaped both his public image and his music. Like many artists from Los Angeles neighborhoods marked by gang politics, poverty, and constant pressure, Slim 400’s life story was inseparable from the environment that produced it.

He emerged not as a polished mainstream figure, but as a rapper whose appeal came from credibility, lived experience, and directness. His music often reflected the codes, dangers, and loyalties of the world he came from. That gave him a particular place in West Coast rap: not necessarily as a crossover pop star, but as a respected street voice whose authenticity mattered deeply to his audience.

Rise in West Coast Street Rap

Slim 400 began building a profile through local records, collaborations, and connections within the wider network of California street rap. His name grew through association with the modern West Coast wave and through a reputation that reached beyond music alone. He represented a branch of rap culture where regional identity, neighborhood affiliation, and biography still carried enormous weight.

As his catalog expanded, he became known for music that felt unfiltered and rooted in firsthand experience. Rather than trying to smooth out his story for commercial comfort, Slim 400 leaned into the harshness of it. That gave his records a rawness that resonated with listeners who saw him as a real product of the streets he described.

Slim 400’s story was never built on distance from danger—it was built on surviving it, documenting it, and carrying it into the music.

Violence, Survival, and Public Resilience

One of the most defining chapters of Slim 400’s life was his survival after being shot multiple times in 2019. That incident reinforced the image many already had of him: not just a rapper talking about danger, but someone living inside it. In rap culture, survival stories can become mythic, and Slim 400’s recovery added a dramatic layer to an identity already shaped by toughness and endurance.

His survival also gave his music a different emotional charge. He was not simply narrating violence from memory or performance. He became a figure whose body itself seemed to carry proof of the risks he rapped about. That reality deepened the respect many supporters had for him and made his story feel especially intense, even within a genre already full of hardship narratives.

Relationships, Reputation, and West Coast Presence

Slim 400’s career was also tied to the broader world of Los Angeles rap relationships, rivalries, affiliations, and public perception. He was part of a scene where artists were often read not only through songs, but through street politics, alliances, and coded references. This made his public life more charged than that of many artists operating in safer commercial spaces.

Even so, Slim 400 maintained a recognizable place in the culture. He became a known Bompton representative and a figure linked to the continuing evolution of West Coast street rap in the streaming era. His impact was grounded less in massive mainstream visibility than in identity, location, and the sense that he stood for something real to the people who followed him.

Death and Loss

On December 8, 2021, Slim 400 was killed in Inglewood, California. His death added his name to the long and painful history of rap artists whose lives ended in violence despite talent, momentum, and survival through earlier danger. For fans and peers, the loss felt especially cruel because his public identity had already become tied to endurance—he had survived before, and many believed he might keep surviving.

His death underscored a hard truth that runs through many hip-hop biographies: escaping one violent chapter does not guarantee escape from the next. In Slim 400’s case, that reality became part of the tragedy of his story and part of what makes his legacy feel so unresolved.

Legacy

Slim 400’s legacy lives in his role as a respected West Coast street voice and a symbolic Bompton figure. He represented a type of rap presence that remains central to hip-hop even when it is not always rewarded with the largest charts: local, real, dangerous, regionally rooted, and deeply tied to lived identity.

He is remembered for resilience, for credibility, and for carrying Los Angeles street experience directly into his art. In that sense, Slim 400 remains part of the ongoing story of West Coast rap, where survival, place, and authenticity still shape who matters.
