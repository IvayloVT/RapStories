Pop Smoke: A Life of Fast-Rising Stardom

Published April 6, 2026 • 6 min read

Early Life and Upbringing

Pop Smoke, born Bashar Barakah Jackson on July 20, 1999, in Brooklyn, New York, came from a family with Caribbean roots. His mother was Jamaican and his father was Panamanian, and that background helped shape both his identity and the confidence he later carried into music. He grew up in the Canarsie neighborhood of Brooklyn, an area that became central to his story and later his public image.

Like many young men raised in difficult city environments, Pop Smoke’s early years were influenced by discipline, ambition, and exposure to the pressures of street life. Before music fully took over, he was interested in sports and had dreams that reached beyond rap. But over time, his path changed, and the world he lived in began to shape both his choices and his art.

Teenage Years and Early Trouble

Pop Smoke’s upbringing was not without setbacks. As a teenager, he had brushes with trouble and spent time around people and situations that reflected the realities of street culture in Brooklyn. Those experiences became part of the authenticity that listeners later heard in his music.

What made him stand out was that he did not present himself as polished or manufactured. From the beginning, he carried a raw presence. His voice, confidence, and style made him feel different from many newer rappers. Even before he became famous, people around him saw charisma, toughness, and star quality.

The Start of His Music Career

Pop Smoke entered rap almost by accident, but once he began recording, it quickly became clear that he had something special. He started building momentum in Brooklyn’s rising drill scene, a local movement inspired by Chicago drill but reshaped with New York energy, UK-style production, and its own street identity.

His breakout came in 2019 with the song “Welcome to the Party”, a record that exploded in clubs, on social media, and in the streets. The song introduced the world to his deep, unmistakable voice and commanding delivery. It was aggressive, catchy, and instantly recognizable. From that moment, Pop Smoke became one of the leading faces of Brooklyn drill.

Rise to Fame

After his breakthrough, Pop Smoke’s rise was rapid. Songs such as “Dior”, “Mood Swings”, and other releases helped him move beyond local fame and into the national spotlight. He had the rare ability to appeal to hardcore street rap listeners while also crossing into mainstream success.

His mixtapes and collaborations expanded his audience quickly. He became known not only for high-energy drill records but also for showing versatility, using melody and smoother production on songs that revealed a broader commercial potential. His sound helped define a new era in New York rap and pushed Brooklyn drill into the mainstream.

Image, Street Ties and Public Perception

As his profile grew, so did public discussion about his street connections and the lifestyle reflected in his music. Like many drill artists, Pop Smoke’s rise came with intense attention to what in his lyrics was performance, what was real, and how much the two overlapped. His music projected power, confidence, and danger, and that image became a major part of his appeal.

At the same time, much of the online conversation around rappers in the drill scene often mixes confirmed facts with rumor and exaggeration. Pop Smoke’s public persona was closely linked to Brooklyn street culture, but his artistic legacy ultimately rests on how powerfully he translated that environment into music.

Why He Became So Important So Fast

Pop Smoke’s success was not only about hit songs. He arrived at a moment when New York rap was searching for a new dominant voice, and he seemed to fill that space almost immediately. His vocal tone was instantly memorable, his beat selection was distinctive, and his presence felt larger than life.

He also had crossover potential. He could make records for the streets, but he also had the charisma and musical instinct to become a global star. Many saw him as one of the most important young rappers of his generation, especially because his growth was happening so quickly.

The Night He Lost His Life

On February 19, 2020, Pop Smoke was killed during a home invasion in the Hollywood Hills in Los Angeles. He had been staying at a rented house when intruders entered the property. During the robbery, he was confronted and shot. His death shocked the music world because he was only 20 years old and his career was still in its early stages.

The killing drew national attention not just because of his fame, but because it reflected how quickly success can collide with danger. Later reporting said several people were charged in the case, including juveniles, and court proceedings continued afterward.

Aftermath and Legacy

Even after his death, Pop Smoke’s influence only grew. His posthumous releases introduced him to an even larger audience, and many listeners began to see him not just as a promising artist, but as a major star whose full potential had been cut short.

He is remembered as one of the defining voices of Brooklyn drill and one of the most important young rappers of his era. His music still carries weight because it captured energy, hunger, ambition, and presence in a way few artists can. His story remains both inspiring and tragic: a young man who rose at extraordinary speed, changed the sound of a city, and lost his life before his career had fully unfolded.
