Pop Smoke

Brooklyn drill superstar whose deep voice, aggressive style, and crossover potential made him one of the defining rap figures of his generation.

Rap Stories Profile

The Story of Pop Smoke

Pop Smoke rose out of Brooklyn with a voice, image, and sound that felt instantly larger than life. In a remarkably short period, he became the face of New York drill, helping transform a local movement into a global rap force. His music carried menace, melody, swagger, and charisma at the same time, and his death cut short what looked like one of the most important careers of the modern era.

Biography

Brooklyn Beginnings

Born Bashar Barakah Jackson, Pop Smoke grew up in Canarsie, Brooklyn, in an environment that shaped both his identity and his music. His roots were Caribbean as well as New York, and that blend helped form the swagger, rhythm, and energy that later made him stand apart. Before rap fully took over, he was known as an athlete and a street-connected young figure whose charisma was obvious long before fame arrived.

The Voice That Changed the Room

What made Pop Smoke feel different from the start was the combination of his sound and presence. His voice was deep, commanding, and instantly recognizable. It gave his music weight before a listener even focused on the lyrics. On top of that voice came a delivery built on confidence, menace, and rhythm. He sounded comfortable over dark, sliding drill production, but he also had the kind of natural star power that suggested he could go much further than one subgenre.

Brooklyn Drill’s Breakout Star

Pop Smoke emerged during the rise of Brooklyn drill, a movement heavily inspired by Chicago drill but reshaped through UK-influenced production and New York energy. He quickly became its leading face. Records such as “Welcome to the Party” gave the movement a breakout anthem, while his mixtape work proved that he was not just a viral name but a real artist with momentum. He made music for clubs, streets, cars, parties, and social media clips all at once.

Pop Smoke sounded like the future of New York rap: gritty, melodic, stylish, dangerous, and made for a global audience.

From Drill Star to Mainstream Force

His rise became even more impressive because he was already moving beyond pure drill. He could make aggressive street records, but he also showed a talent for melody and crossover structure. Songs like “Dior” turned him into a major cultural presence, while later records revealed an artist expanding into radio, hooks, and broader commercial appeal without losing his identity. He looked ready to become a superstar, not just a scene leader.

Style, Image, and Cultural Impact

Pop Smoke’s appeal was larger than music alone. He carried himself like a star in a classic sense: jewelry, confidence, fashion, movement, and attitude all felt deliberate. He represented a new generation of New York rap that was less interested in nostalgia and more interested in taking control of the moment. He helped bring New York back into a center-stage conversation during a period when many critics felt the city had lost its dominance in rap’s national direction.

A Career Cut Short

In 2020, Pop Smoke was killed in Los Angeles at just 20 years old. His death shocked the music world because it came at the very moment his rise appeared unstoppable. What made the loss especially heavy was how much potential still seemed ahead of him. He had already become the face of a movement, but the evidence suggested he was on track to become something even bigger: a defining mainstream rap star with long-term crossover power.

Legacy

Pop Smoke’s legacy remains powerful because his impact was immediate and unmistakable. He gave Brooklyn drill its clearest superstar, helped reshape the national conversation around New York rap, and left behind music that still feels alive, cinematic, and full of force. Even with a short career, he built the kind of presence many artists spend decades chasing. His story is one of explosive rise, unfinished greatness, and a lasting influence on the sound of modern rap.
